# 📖 ML Glossary — Simple Definitions for Beginners

> Stuck on a word? Find it here. All definitions are written in plain English — no jargon!

---

## A

**Accuracy**
The percentage of predictions your model got right.
*Example: 90% accuracy means 90 out of 100 predictions were correct.*

**Algorithm**
A set of step-by-step instructions a computer follows to solve a problem.
*Think of it like a recipe: follow the steps and you get the result.*

**Array**
A list of numbers arranged in rows and columns. NumPy arrays are like Excel tables made of numbers.

---

## B

**Backpropagation**
How a neural network learns from its mistakes — it looks at the error and adjusts the weights going backward through the network.

**Bias (in ML models)**
When a model is too simple and consistently gets predictions wrong in the same direction. *A model with high bias is "underfitting."*

**Binary Classification**
Predicting one of two options. *Example: Is this email spam (yes/no)?*

---

## C

**Classification**
Predicting which *category* something belongs to.
*Example: Is this photo a cat or a dog?*

**Clustering**
Grouping similar items together without being told the labels.
*Example: Grouping customers by shopping habits.*

**Confusion Matrix**
A table that shows how many predictions were right or wrong, broken down by each category. Helps you understand *where* your model makes mistakes.

**Cross-Validation**
A technique to fairly measure how well your model works by testing it on multiple different parts of the data.

---

## D

**Dataset**
A collection of data your model learns from.
*Think of it as a spreadsheet full of examples.*

**Decision Tree**
A model that makes predictions by asking a series of yes/no questions.
*Like a flowchart for making decisions.*

**Deep Learning**
A type of ML using neural networks with many layers. It's powerful for images, audio, and text.

**Dimensionality**
The number of features (columns) in your data. High dimensionality = many features.

---

## E

**Epoch**
One complete pass through all the training data during neural network training.
*Like reading a textbook from cover to cover once.*

**Evaluation**
Measuring how well your model performs, usually on data it hasn't seen before.

---

## F

**Feature**
An input variable used to make predictions. In a housing dataset, features might be: number of rooms, location, age of house.

**Feature Engineering**
Creating new, more useful features from existing ones.
*Example: Creating "price per square foot" from "price" and "area."*

**Fitting**
Training a model on data so it learns the patterns.

---

## H

**Hyperparameter**
Settings you choose *before* training a model (unlike parameters, which the model learns itself).
*Example: How deep should a decision tree be? That's a hyperparameter.*

---

## L

**Label**
The "answer" or output you're trying to predict.
*In a spam detector, the label is "spam" or "not spam."*

**Learning Rate**
How quickly a model adjusts its weights during training. Too high = overshoots; too low = too slow.

**Loss Function**
A way to measure how wrong the model's predictions are. The goal is to minimize this.

---

## M

**Model**
The result of training an algorithm on data — it's what makes predictions.
*Think of it as the "learned brain" that predicts new things.*

**MSE (Mean Squared Error)**
A way to measure how far predictions are from the real values (used in regression). Lower is better.

---

## N

**Neural Network**
A system of connected nodes (like brain neurons) that learns patterns through training.

**Normalization**
Scaling numbers to a standard range (like 0 to 1) so no single feature dominates.

---

## O

**Overfitting**
When a model learns the training data *too well* — including its noise and quirks — and performs poorly on new data.
*Like memorizing past exam questions instead of understanding the subject.*

---

## P

**Parameter**
Values the model learns during training (different from hyperparameters which you set manually).
*In linear regression, the slope and intercept are parameters.*

**PCA (Principal Component Analysis)**
A technique to reduce many features down to fewer, while keeping most of the useful information.

**Precision**
Out of all the times the model said "yes," how many times was it actually right?

**Prediction**
The model's output for new, unseen data.

---

## R

**Random Forest**
A collection of many decision trees that vote together to make a better prediction.

**Recall**
Out of all the actual "yes" cases, how many did the model correctly find?

**Regression**
Predicting a *continuous number* (like price, temperature, salary).

**R² Score (R-squared)**
Tells you how much of the variation in the data your model explains. 1.0 = perfect; 0 = no better than guessing the average.

---

## S

**Supervised Learning**
Learning from labeled examples — where you know the "right answer" for each training example.

**SVM (Support Vector Machine)**
A model that finds the best boundary line between two groups, maximizing the gap between them.

---

## T

**Test Set**
Data held back from training, used only to evaluate the final model. The model never sees this during training.

**Training**
The process of showing a model examples so it learns patterns.

**Training Set**
The data used to train (teach) the model.

---

## U

**Underfitting**
When a model is too simple to capture the patterns in data. It performs poorly on both training and test data.

**Unsupervised Learning**
Learning without labels — the model finds patterns on its own.

---

## V

**Validation Set**
A separate portion of data used to tune hyperparameters during training (different from the test set).

**Variance (in ML models)**
When a model is too sensitive to the training data and performs very differently on new data. *A model with high variance is "overfitting."*

---

## W

**Weight**
A number inside a neural network that gets adjusted during training. Weights determine how much influence each input has.
